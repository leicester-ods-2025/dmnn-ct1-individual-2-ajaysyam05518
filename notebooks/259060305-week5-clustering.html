1 1. Introduction

This analysis employs clustering techniques on the Online Retail dataset to identify behavioural structures within the data. Clustering does not depend on a target variable, unlike predictive modelling tasks. It groups observations together based on how similar their features are.

Clustering is based on distance, so decisions about how to model things, like what unit of analysis to use, how to build features, and how to scale things, have a direct effect on the structure that comes out. So, it’s important to carefully explain why these choices were made.

The aim of this report is to create, put into action, and critically analyse a customer-level clustering framework that works for retail behavioural segmentation.

2 2. Unit of Analysis

The customer (Customer ID level) is the unit of analysis that was chosen. Each observation in the clustering dataset is a single customer whose behaviour is described by a set of aggregated behavioural traits based on their transaction history.

Customer-level clustering is suitable since retail segmentation is fundamentally centred on the customer. Most businesses want to know the difference between high-value customers, customers who buy a lot, and customers who might not be interested anymore. Grouping transactions or invoices together would show short-term buying patterns instead of long-term behaviour patterns.

Combining data at the customer level cuts down on noise from individual transactions and lets consistent patterns of behaviour show up. This modelling choice sets the shape of the clustering space: it looks at how similar customers are based on things like how often, how much, and how recently they spend money.

3 3. Data Preparation

Before making behavioural features, the Online Retail dataset needs to be cleaned up so that clustering shows real buying behaviour instead of data artefacts. Retail transaction data often has invoices that were cancelled, customer IDs that are missing, and wrong amounts. If these problems aren’t fixed, they can mess up calculations of money and behaviour.

Here are the steps for cleaning:

  • Take out cancelled invoices (those with invoice numbers that start with “C”).
  • Take out observations that don’t have a Customer ID.
  • Get rid of quantities that aren’t positive.- Make a variable for the total price of each transaction.

These steps make sure that the next step, customer-level aggregation, makes sense from an economic point of view and is consistent within the company.

import pandas as pd
import numpy as np

# Load the dataset
df = pd.read_csv("online_retail_II.csv", encoding="ISO-8859-1")

# Convert InvoiceDate to datetime
df["InvoiceDate"] = pd.to_datetime(df["InvoiceDate"], dayfirst=True)

# Remove cancelled invoices
df = df[~df["Invoice"].astype(str).str.startswith("C")]

# Remove missing Customer ID
df = df.dropna(subset=["Customer ID"])

# Remove zero or negative quantities
df = df[df["Quantity"] > 0]

# Create TotalPrice variable
df["TotalPrice"] = df["Quantity"] * df["Price"]

# Display dataset shape after cleaning

After cleaning, the dataset only has valid transactions linked to customers who can be identified. Removing cancelled invoices stops reversed transactions from making total spending seem lower than it really is. Not including missing Customer IDs makes sure that the customer-level data is grouped correctly. Removing non-positive amounts stops wrong money calculations.

This cleaned dataset is the basis for building behavioural features.

4 4. Feature Construction

How observations are represented is the only thing that affects clustering performance. Because the customer is the unit of analysis, transaction-level data needs to be combined into behavioural summaries.

The goal is to make numeric features that give a good picture of how customers buy things. Four characteristics are defined: Total Spend, Purchase Frequency, Average Basket Value, and Recency. These four things together show how much money someone has given, how involved they are, how they buy things, and what they do over time.

4.1 4.1 Total Spend

Total Spend represents the cumulative monetary value generated by each customer during the observation period.

[ _i = (Quantity Price) ]

This feature captures overall economic contribution and differentiates high-value customers from low-value customers.

Limitation:
Total Spend may be influenced by extreme purchasers and does not distinguish between frequent small purchases and occasional large purchases.

4.2 4.2 Purchase Frequency

Purchase Frequency counts the number of different invoices that each customer has. This feature shows how often people buy things and how engaged they are with the brand.

Limitation: Frequency doesn’t take into account the size of the transaction and may treat small and large purchases the same.

4.3 4.3 Average Basket Value

Average Basket Value is calculated as:

[ _i = ]

This shows how much people spend on each visit and separates premium shoppers from those who buy less.

Limitation:
This metric may not be stable for customers who don’t buy very often.

4.4 4.4 Recency

Recency tells you how many days have passed since a customer last bought something compared to the most recent date in the dataset. This shows how fresh engagement is and how likely it is to end.

Limitation: The observation window affects recency, and it doesn’t show purchase history outside of the dataset.

# Set reference date (latest transaction date)
reference_date = df["InvoiceDate"].max()

# Aggregate transaction data to customer level
customer_df = df.groupby("Customer ID").agg(
    TotalSpend=("TotalPrice", "sum"),
    Frequency=("Invoice", "nunique"),
    LastPurchase=("InvoiceDate", "max")
)

# Compute Recency
customer_df["Recency"] = (reference_date - customer_df["LastPurchase"]).dt.days

# Compute Average Basket Value
customer_df["AvgBasketValue"] = customer_df["TotalSpend"] / customer_df["Frequency"]

# Drop LastPurchase column
customer_df = customer_df.drop(columns=["LastPurchase"])

5 5. Scaling

K-means and other clustering algorithms use Euclidean distance to find similarities between observations. When features are measured on different scales, the ones with bigger numbers have a bigger effect on the distance calculation.

In the dataset that was made, TotalSpend can be in the thousands, but Frequency and Recency are usually much smaller. If you don’t scale, the clustering algorithm would mostly group customers based on how much they spent, which would mean that it would ignore other behavioural dimensions.

Scaling makes sure that each feature adds the right amount to the distance metric. This stops one behaviour from having too much of an effect on cluster formation and keeps the feature space’s geometric balance.

This analysis uses standardisation (z-score scaling). Each feature is changed so that it has a mean of zero and a standard deviation of one: [ z = ] This transformation ensures that clustering reflects relative behavioural differences rather than absolute magnitudes.

from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

X = customer_df[features]

# Apply standardisation
scaler = StandardScaler()

6 6. K-Means Clustering

K-means clustering divides observations into ( k ) groups by making the variance within each group as small as possible. The algorithm puts each observation in the cluster center that is closest to it based on Euclidean distance. It then updates the cluster centers until they all come together.

Choosing the right value of ( k ) is an important modelling decision because K-means needs to know how many clusters there are ahead of time. Instead of picking a random number, we look at a range of ( k ) values and look at the Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) to see if adding more complexity to the model is worth it.

from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

wcss = []

for k in range(1, 9):
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=k, random_state=42, n_init=20)
    km.fit(X_scaled)
    wcss.append(km.inertia_)

plt.plot(range(1, 9), wcss, marker='o')
plt.xlabel("Number of Clusters (k)")
plt.ylabel("Within-Cluster Sum of Squares")
plt.title("Elbow Method")
plt.show()

The Within-Cluster Sum of Squares (WCSS) goes down as the number of clusters goes up because the clusters get smaller. But usually, the rate of improvement slows down after a certain point. This “elbow” shows how to find a middle ground between making models more complicated and making them easier to understand.

The selected value of ( k ) is determined by identifying the point at which the addition of extra clusters yields progressively smaller decreases in WCSS while preserving interpretability.

# Final K-means model (example: k=3)
kmeans_model = KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42, n_init=20)
kmeans_model.fit(X_scaled)

# Add cluster labels to dataset
customer_df["Cluster"] = kmeans_model.labels_

For visualisation purposes, PCA reduces the high-dimensional behavioural space to two main components. So, overlapping clusters in two dimensions don’t always mean that the original space isn’t well separated.

7.4 Critical Evaluation

The clusters seem to show differences in behaviour mostly when it comes to spending and engagement. K-means, on the other hand, assumes that clusters are spherical and that the variance is the same across groups. This assumption may distort segmentation if behavioural patterns are highly skewed or non-spherical.

8 8. Sensitivity and Limitations

Clustering results are not objective truths; they are outcomes shaped by modelling assumptions and design choices. It is important to recognise a number of limitations. 1. The results depend on the number of clusters ( k ) that you choose. The elbow method helps, but it doesn’t give a single correct value. Different values of ( k ) may yield distinct yet viable segmentations. 2. K-means thinks that the clusters are spherical and tries to minimise the variance within each cluster using Euclidean distance. K-means may create fake boundaries if the real behavioural structure is irregular or based on density. 3. The results of clustering depend a lot on how the features are built. The selected behavioural variables (Total Spend, Frequency, Recency, and Average Basket Value) delineate similarity. Different structural patterns might come from using different feature representations, like product diversity or seasonal behaviour. 4. Decisions about scaling have an effect on geometry. Standardisation equalises feature influence, but it also presupposes that all behavioural dimensions should contribute equally to similarity. In real life, some dimensions may be more important to the economy than others. 5. The analysis is only valid for the time period that was looked at. The available dataset measures recent purchases and spending habits, but it may not show a customer’s long-term history. These limitations underscore that clustering is a modelling endeavour rather than an absolute segmentation veracity. Interpretation must, therefore, remain prudent and cognisant of context.

9 9. Conclusion

This analysis utilised clustering techniques on the Online Retail dataset to identify behavioural patterns among customers. By choosing the customer as the unit of analysis, putting transaction-level data into meaningful behavioural features, and carefully using standardisation, a structured picture of buying behaviour was made.

We used K-means clustering to divide customers into different behavioural groups based on how much they spend, how often they buy, how recently they bought something, and how much their basket is worth. We looked at cluster centers in the original scale so that we could understand them in a way that made sense economically. We also used PCA to show structure in smaller dimensions.

The clusters that come out show differences in how engaged customers are and how much money they make. But these segments shouldn’t be taken as absolute truths. They show how the modeller chose which features to use, how to scale them, and what assumptions to make about the algorithm.

In general, this task shows that clustering isn’t about finding one “correct” number of clusters; it’s about understanding how the assumptions we make about representation and modelling affect the structure that comes out. Critical interpretation is thus essential to effective segmentation.
